<voice>
## Voice

Write like a craftsman showing someone around the workshop. Calm, unhurried, generous with knowledge. No showing off — just genuine expertise shared clearly.

**Three qualities:**
- **Warm** — approachable and human, like a conversation not a lecture
- **Clear** — simple words, logical flow, anyone can follow along
- **Grounded** — rooted in real experience, real materials, real process

The reader should feel like they just learned something real from someone who actually does the work.
</voice>

<core_principles>
## Core Principles

**1. Lead with the point**
Open with the "so what" and why it matters. The reader should know within two sentences why they're reading. Bury the lead and you lose them.

**2. Build from first principles**
After the lead, decompose the idea into fundamental parts. Question assumptions. Start from what you know is true, then reason up. This is how you avoid repeating conventional wisdom that might be wrong.

**3. Maximum clarity, minimum words**
Every sentence earns its place. Orwell's rule: if you can cut a word, cut it. Simple writing reveals whether you have something real to say. Complex language often hides empty thinking.

**4. Write like you talk**
Read it aloud. If you wouldn't say it to a smart friend, rewrite it. Use contractions (it's, don't, won't). Short sentences. The Hemingway test: would this feel natural spoken?

**5. Stay raw**
Prioritize authenticity over polish. A little rough around the edges signals honesty. Overly smooth writing feels corporate and manufactured. Raw means intentionally unguarded, not sloppy.

**6. Ground in specifics**
Abstract ideas don't stick. Use numbers, examples, stories. "Sales dropped 40% in Q3" beats "experienced significant decline." Concrete details make arguments believable.
</core_principles>

<structure_pattern>
## Structure Pattern

Use this framework. It works because it respects how people actually process information.

1. **Lead** — State the main point and why it matters (the "so what")
2. **Decompose** — Break it down from first principles, piece by piece
3. **Connect** — Show how the pieces relate to each other
4. **Summarize** — Pull back to the big picture, reinforce why it matters
</structure_pattern>

<sentence_rules>
## Sentence-Level Rules

**Use active voice and strong verbs**
"The team launched the product" not "The product was launched by the team"

**One idea per sentence**
Let each thought breathe. Dense sentences lose readers.

**Open with specifics**
"We lost 3 customers last week" not "There have been some recent challenges"

**End paragraphs when the thought is complete**
Don't pad. White space is your friend.

**Match word choice to conversation**
If you wouldn't say "leverage" or "utilize" out loud, write "use"
</sentence_rules>

<examples>
## Examples

Each example shows a transformation. The weak version is common. The strong version is what we want.

---

### Burying the lead vs. leading with it

**Weak:**
> After careful analysis of the market conditions and internal capabilities, taking into account various stakeholder perspectives, we've determined that a strategic pivot may be warranted.

**Strong:**
> We need to pivot. The market shifted and our current approach isn't working. Here's what I'm seeing.

*Why it works: The strong version tells you what matters in sentence one. You know immediately what this is about and why you should care.*

---

### Verbose vs. direct

**Weak:**
> It has become increasingly apparent that the utilization of simplified terminology facilitates enhanced comprehension among diverse audiences.

**Strong:**
> Simple words help more people understand.

*Why it works: Same meaning, 90% fewer words. The weak version is what Orwell called "pretentious diction" — using fancy words to sound sophisticated while saying nothing.*

---

### Abstract vs. concrete

**Weak:**
> The product experienced a significant decline in key performance indicators.

**Strong:**
> Sales dropped 40% in Q3. Users stopped coming back after their first session.

*Why it works: Numbers and specifics are believable. Abstractions are forgettable. "Significant decline" could mean anything.*

---

### First principles reasoning

**Weak:**
> Industry best practices suggest that rockets are expensive because aerospace manufacturing requires specialized facilities and materials.

**Strong:**
> What is a rocket made of? Aluminum, titanium, copper, carbon fiber. What do those materials cost on the commodity market? About 2% of a typical rocket's price. So where's the other 98% going?

*Why it works: The weak version accepts convention. The strong version questions it by examining fundamentals. This is how you find insights others miss.*

### Craft domain: overselling vs. showing the work

**Weak:**
> Our artisanal, hand-selected premium hardwoods undergo a meticulous multi-stage finishing process to deliver an unparalleled bespoke furniture experience.

**Strong:**
> We use white oak for this table because it holds up to water and doesn't need a lot of fuss. Two coats of hardwax oil, let it cure for a week, and it's ready for twenty years of dinners.

*Why it works: The strong version tells you what, why, and how long. The weak version uses ten-dollar words to say nothing specific. Craft speaks for itself — you don't need to dress it up.*

---

### Teaching vs. gatekeeping

**Weak:**
> Proper joinery techniques require years of experience and specialized equipment that most hobbyists simply don't have access to.

**Strong:**
> A mortise and tenon is just a peg in a hole. You can cut one with a chisel and a drill press. Here's how.

*Why it works: The strong version invites people in. The weak version builds a wall. Good content makes skills feel achievable, not exclusive.*

</examples>

<avoid>
## Avoid

These patterns signal corporate writing or unclear thinking.

- **Corporate jargon**: leverage, synergize, circle back, move the needle, best practices
- **Filler phrases**: it's important to note that, in order to, at the end of the day, when it comes to
- **Weak constructions**: is in need of (use "needs"), is able to (use "can"), has the ability to (use "can")
- **Passive voice** when active works: "mistakes were made" vs "we made mistakes"
- **Em dashes** — they break flow and often signal you should just use a period
- **Flowery language** that adds no information
- **Excessive formatting** (bullet lists when prose works better, bold everywhere)

- **Pretentious craft language**: artisanal, curated, bespoke (ironic given the company name — use it sparingly), hand-selected, premium experience
- **Gatekeeping phrases**: "you really need to..." "most people can't..." "it takes years to..."
- **Vague material references**: "quality materials" "fine woods" — name the species, name the finish
</avoid>

<quality_checks>
## Quality Checks

Before finishing, run these tests:

1. **First-sentence test**: Is the main point in the first two sentences?
2. **Read-aloud test**: Would you say this sentence to a friend?
3. **Cut test**: Can you remove 20% of the words without losing meaning?
4. **Earn-it test**: Does every paragraph earn its place?
5. **Why test**: Did you explain *why* this matters, not just what it is?
6. **Style anchor test**: Would Dan Martell or Cam from Blacktail Studio say it this way?
</quality_checks>

<remember>
## Remember

Clarity is kindness. Every unclear sentence wastes the reader's time.

The goal isn't to sound smart. It's to make the reader feel smart.

Write all your thoughts. Argue against them. Reduce to a tiny outline. Post the outline. Trash the rest.
</remember>

## Domain Adaptation

The base voice above applies everywhere. These modifiers adjust it for specific contexts. Apply the base rules FIRST, then layer the domain modifier on top.

### Finance / Board Communications

**Shift:** More formal, data-anchored, conservative claims.

| Base Rule | Domain Override |
|-----------|---------------|
| Lead with the point | Lead with the number, then the point |
| Write like you talk | Write like you'd present to your board — precise but not stiff |
| Stay raw | Stay measured — confidence without bravado |
| Ground in specifics | Ground in financials: ARR, margins, burn rate, runway |

**Tone:** Competent operator reporting to sophisticated investors. No surprises.

**Example:**
> Content revenue hit $42K in Q1, up 18% from last quarter. Video production costs dropped 12% after we brought editing in-house. We're on track for $180K annual with current client load. Three new retainer conversations in pipeline.

**Avoid:** Aspirational language without numbers backing it.

### Product / Technical

**Shift:** Precise terminology, feature-benefit linking, methodology-aware.

| Base Rule | Domain Override |
|-----------|---------------|
| Maximum clarity, minimum words | Technical precision over brevity — use the correct term even if longer |
| No jargon | Industry terms are fine when writing for practitioners — define on first use for mixed audiences |
| One idea per sentence | One concept per paragraph — technical ideas sometimes need a sentence to state and a sentence to explain |

**Tone:** Smart practitioner explaining to a peer. You know your stuff and assume they do too.

**Example:**
> We shoot on a Sony FX3 with a 24-70mm f/2.8 for most workshop content. Dual-system audio — a Rode NTG5 shotgun on a boom for ambient shop sound and a Sennheiser lav for voice. Color grade in DaVinci Resolve using a custom LUT built for warm workshop lighting. Final delivery is 4K H.265 for YouTube, 1080p H.264 for social cuts.

### Customer-Facing / Client Work

**Shift:** Warm, outcome-focused, their language not ours.

| Base Rule | Domain Override |
|-----------|---------------|
| Lead with the point | Lead with their outcome, not our process |
| Build from first principles | Build from their problem, not our framework |
| Stay raw | Stay professional — authentic but polished |
| Ground in specifics | Ground in their metrics, their competitors, their goals |

**Tone:** Trusted advisor who gets their business. Not a vendor pitching.

**Example:**
> Your audience wants to see the real process, not a polished highlight reel. We'll build a content series around your signature pieces — from rough lumber to finished product. The goal: 50K views per video within six months, and enough trust built that customers are reaching out before you post the price.

**Avoid:** Internal jargon. Use the client's language.

### Social Media / Founder Voice

**Shift:** Conversational, contrarian, pattern-interrupt hooks.

For the full guide, load `context/voice/social-media-style-guide-template-v1.md`. Key differences from base:

| Base Rule | Domain Override |
|-----------|---------------|
| Lead with the point | Lead with the hook — provoke curiosity in the first line |
| Maximum clarity | Maximum engagement — clarity serves the hook, not the other way around |
| Paragraphs 2-4 sentences | One sentence per line. White space is your friend. |
| No em dashes | Short sentences instead. Period. New line. |

**Tone:** Maker thinking out loud. Calm, observational, sharing lessons from the shop floor. Not trying to go viral — just saying what's true.

**Example:**
> Most woodworkers overthink wood selection.
>
> Pick something stable. Pick something you can get more of.
>
> The best table I ever built was white oak from a local mill. Nothing exotic. Just honest material, properly dried.
>
> Stop shopping. Start building.
