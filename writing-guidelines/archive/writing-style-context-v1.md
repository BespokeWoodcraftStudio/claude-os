# Writing Style Context

**Use for:** External writing (blogs, newsletters, product communication, thought leadership). Not for legal, compliance, or highly formal contexts.

## Voice

Write like a smart friend explaining something important over coffee. This means: direct (no jargon), clear (simple words), real (authentic, not polished).

Think Paul Graham's clarity, Naval Ravikant's compression, Derek Sivers' rawness.

---

## Core Principles

**Lead with the point.** Open with the "so what" and why it matters. The reader should know within two sentences why they're reading.

**Build from first principles.** After the lead, decompose the idea into its fundamental parts. Question assumptions. Connect the dots. Then zoom out to the full picture.

**Maximum clarity, minimum words.** Every sentence earns its place. Cut ruthlessly. If a word doesn't add meaning, delete it.

**Stay conversational.** Write how you'd explain it out loud. Short sentences. Direct language. No words you wouldn't say in real conversation.

**Keep it raw.** Prioritize authenticity over polish. A little rough around the edges is fine. Overly smooth writing feels corporate and fake. Raw means authentic and unguarded, not unedited or sloppy. The roughness is intentional craft.

---

## Structure Pattern

1. **Lead** - State the main point and why it matters
2. **Decompose** - Break it down from first principles
3. **Connect** - Show how the pieces relate
4. **Summarize** - Pull back to the big picture

---

## Tone Calibration

**Write like these authors:**
- Paul Graham: plain rhetoric, short sentences. *"The way to get startup ideas is not to try to think of startup ideas."*
- Naval Ravikant: compressed wisdom, zero filler. *"Seek wealth, not money or status. Wealth is assets that earn while you sleep."*
- Derek Sivers: pithy insights, surprising angles. *"Most people don't know why they're doing what they're doing. They imitate others."*
- Morgan Housel: storytelling + wit. *"The highest form of wealth is the ability to wake up every morning and say, 'I can do whatever I want today.'"*
- James Clear: actionable clarity. *"You do not rise to the level of your goals. You fall to the level of your systems."*

**The vibe:** Smart but not pretentious. Urgent but not anxious. Direct but emotionally intelligent.

---

## Do This

- Use active voice and strong verbs
- Open with specifics, not abstractions
- Ground ideas in concrete examples
- Write one idea per sentence
- Use contractions (it's, don't, won't)
- End paragraphs when the thought is complete
- Show genuine understanding of the reader's situation

---

## Avoid This

- Corporate jargon ("leverage," "synergize," "circle back")
- Em dashes
- Filler phrases ("it's important to note that," "in order to")
- Weak verb constructions ("is in need of" instead of "needs")
- Passive voice when active works
- Flowery language that adds no information
- Sentences that wouldn't sound natural spoken aloud
- Excessive formatting (bullet lists when prose works better)

## Examples

### Burying the lead vs. leading with it

**Weak:**
> After careful analysis of the market conditions and internal capabilities, taking into account various stakeholder perspectives, we've determined that a strategic pivot may be warranted.

**Strong:**
> We need to pivot. The market shifted and our current approach isn't working. Here's what I'm seeing...

---

### Verbose vs. direct

**Weak:**
> It has become increasingly apparent that the utilization of simplified terminology facilitates enhanced comprehension among diverse audiences.

**Strong:**
> Simple words help more people understand.

---

### Abstract vs. concrete

**Weak:**
> The product experienced a significant decline in key performance indicators.

**Strong:**
> Sales dropped 40% in Q3. Users stopped coming back after their first session.

---

### Building from first principles

**Weak:**
> Industry best practices suggest that rockets are expensive because aerospace manufacturing requires specialized facilities and materials.

**Strong:**
> What is a rocket made of? Aluminum, titanium, copper, carbon fiber. What do those materials cost on the commodity market? About 2% of a typical rocket's price. So where's the other 98% going?

---

## Quick Checks

Before finishing, ask:

1. Is the main point in the first two sentences?
2. Would I say this sentence out loud?
3. Can I cut 20% of the words?
4. Does every paragraph earn its place?
5. Did I explain *why* this matters, not just what it is?
6. Would Paul Graham write it this way?

---

## Remember

Clarity is kindness. Every unclear sentence wastes the reader's time.

The goal isn't to sound smart. It's to make the reader feel smart.
