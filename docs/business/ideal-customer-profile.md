## TL;DR

- Our best clients are small-to-mid woodworking businesses doing great work with little or no online presence.
- They need to be making enough revenue to invest $3k-$5k/month in content without it being a financial stretch.
- The single biggest predictor of success: the maker genuinely wants to share their process and is willing to be on camera (or at least let us film their hands and shop).
- We struggle with hobbyists, people who want to micromanage edits, and anyone expecting viral results in 30 days.
- Say no early. A bad-fit client costs more than an empty slot.

---

## The Point

Not every woodworker who wants content should work with us.

ICP discipline matters because a bad-fit client drains our best people. When the fit is wrong — say, a hobbyist who isn't generating revenue, or a maker who wants to approve every cut before we edit — we spend all our time managing the relationship instead of making great content. The work suffers, they're unhappy, we're unhappy, and the referral never comes. A single wrong-fit client can cost us two right-fit ones.

---

## The Short Version

**We work best with:**

- Professional woodworkers or makers with an established business (not a side hobby)
- Revenue of $200k+/year — enough to invest in content as a growth channel, not a luxury
- Visually compelling work — furniture, cabinetry, turning, boat building, timber framing, or similar crafts that look great on camera
- A willingness to be filmed (even if camera-shy) and to trust the creative process
- A single decision-maker who can approve content without a committee

**We struggle with:**

- Hobbyists or early-stage makers without consistent revenue
- Makers who want to control every edit and shot (micromanagers)
- Businesses expecting immediate ROI — content compounds over months, not days
- Clients who are hard to schedule or frequently cancel shoot days

---

## Why These Criteria Matter

### Established Business with Real Revenue

A maker doing $200k+ in revenue treats content as a business investment. They understand that $3k-$5k/month is a growth expense, not a gamble. When a client is financially stretched, every invoice becomes a stress point, and they're more likely to churn before content has time to compound. We want clients who see content as infrastructure, not a luxury.

### Visually Compelling Craft

Not all content is created equal. A furniture maker hand-cutting dovetails gives us footage that stops the scroll. A maker whose work is mostly hidden (installation, repair) is harder to showcase. We can work with almost any trade in the woodworking space, but the content works best when the craft is inherently visual — shavings flying, grain revealing, joints fitting tight.

### Willingness to Be on Camera

The best-performing content in the maker space shows the person behind the work. Clients don't need to be polished speakers — we handle the editing and storytelling. But they do need to let us into their shop, show their process, and occasionally talk about why they do what they do. If someone flat-out refuses to appear in any form, the content ceiling is much lower.

### Single Decision-Maker

Content moves fast. We can't wait two weeks for a three-person partnership to agree on a thumbnail. Our best clients have one person who says "looks good, ship it" — and that trust is built during the starter package. Committee-driven approvals slow everything down and kill creative momentum.

## The Scoring System

We use a simple three-stage qualification to decide if a prospect is the right fit.

| Stage | Max Points | Minimum to Proceed |
|-------|------------|-------------------|
| Business Fit (revenue, craft type, location) | 30 | 20 |
| Content Readiness (camera comfort, availability, existing presence) | 40 | 25 |
| Working Style (decision-making, trust, communication) | 30 | 20 |

**Final score determines the path:**

| Score | Decision |
|-------|----------|
| 80-100 | Green light — pitch the starter package immediately |
| 65-79 | Good fit — proceed, but set clear expectations on timeline and process |
| 50-64 | Borderline — have an honest conversation about readiness before offering a starter |
| Below 50 | Not ready — offer to reconnect in 6 months, share some DIY tips in the meantime |

---

## Red Flags

Walk away if you see:

- **"Can you guarantee I'll go viral?"** — Signals unrealistic expectations and a misunderstanding of how content growth works
- **"I need to approve every clip before you edit"** — Signals a micromanagement style that will grind production to a halt
- **"I can only film on one specific day every other month"** — Signals scheduling friction that makes consistent content impossible
- **"My business partner / spouse / investor also needs to approve everything"** — Signals committee decision-making that will slow the entire process

## The Bottom Line

Our best customers share a few things:

1. They're proud of their craft and want the world to see it
2. They have a real business generating real revenue — content is a growth investment, not a Hail Mary
3. They trust us to handle the creative side while they stay in the shop
4. They show up on shoot days, ready to work
5. They think in months and quarters, not days — they understand content compounds

ICP discipline is how we win. Say no fast so you can say yes with force.
