## Synthesis Notes

### What emerged from the research

**Pattern 1: The best woodworking creators aren't just teachers — they're characters.**
Every top creator has a distinct persona that colors their instruction. Paul Sellers doesn't just show you how to sharpen a chisel; he does it with the patience of a man who has sharpened ten thousand chisels. Bourbon Moth doesn't just build a table; he builds it while making you laugh at his mistakes. The voice IS the content.

**Pattern 2: Authority comes from transparency, not perfection.**
The creators gaining the most trust in 2025-2026 are the ones who show their failures (Bourbon Moth, April Wilkerson, Pask Makes). This is a shift from the "master craftsman reveals secrets" model. The new model is "skilled maker invites you into the process, warts and all."

**Pattern 3: The craft-philosophy connection is what separates good from great.**
Essential Craftsman, Nick Offerman, and Wood By Wright don't just teach technique — they teach *why craft matters*. This philosophical layer is what turns a how-to article from a Google search result into something people bookmark and share.

**Pattern 4: Visual storytelling translates to written storytelling.**
Ishitani's silent videos, Chris Salomone's cinematic approach, and Frank Howarth's stop-motion all demonstrate that HOW you present the craft is as important as WHAT you present. In writing, this means: scene-setting, sensory detail, pacing, and emotional arc.

## The 10 Archetype Model — Refined

After cross-referencing creator profiles with brand archetype theory (Jung/12 archetypes) and publication voice analysis, here's the framework:

### Tier 1: Core How-To Voices (use these most)

1. **The Patient Master** — Deep expertise shared without ego. Precise, warm, unhurried.
   - *Written voice:* "Here's what I've learned over 30 years. Let me walk you through it."
   - *Creators:* Paul Sellers, Marc Spagnuolo
   - *Best for:* Technique fundamentals, tool guides, beginner instruction

2. **The Friendly Democratizer** — Removes barriers, celebrates simplicity, says "you can do this."
   - *Written voice:* "Forget what the forums say you need. Here's what actually works."
   - *Creators:* Steve Ramsey, Rex Krueger
   - *Best for:* Budget builds, beginner projects, tool alternatives

3. **The Honest Maker** — Vulnerable, funny, shows the mess. Builds trust through imperfection.
   - *Written voice:* "I screwed this up three times before I figured it out. Here's the shortcut."
   - *Creators:* Bourbon Moth, April Wilkerson
   - *Best for:* Relatable how-tos, mistake-prevention, "what I wish I knew" articles

### Tier 2: Elevation Voices (blend these in for depth)

4. **The Narrative Craftsman** — Every project is a story. Context, motivation, arc.
   - *Written voice:* "This table started as a phone call from a couple who'd just lost their elm tree."
   - *Creators:* Blacktail Studio, Chris Salomone
   - *Best for:* Project features, client stories, material sourcing narratives

5. **The Philosophical Tradesman** — Craft as meaning. Why we make things matters.
   - *Written voice:* "There's something about holding a tool that someone made by hand a century ago."
   - *Creators:* Essential Craftsman, Nick Offerman
   - *Best for:* Opinion pieces, craft philosophy, "why woodworking" articles

6. **The Poetic Explorer** — Learning in public. Wonder and curiosity as teaching tools.
   - *Written voice:* "I'd never tried Kumiko before. Here's what happened when I did."
   - *Creators:* Pask Makes, Michael Alm
   - *Best for:* New technique explorations, experimental builds, journey articles

### Tier 3: Specialist Voices (use for specific content types)

7. **The Silent Master** — Let the work speak. Minimal words, maximum craft.
   - *Written voice:* Photo essays. Step shots with single-sentence captions. No chatter.
   - *Creators:* Ishitani
   - *Best for:* Photo-heavy tutorials, Instagram content, visual guides

8. **The Bridge Builder** — Connects traditions, cultures, old and new.
   - *Written voice:* "Western woodworkers push the saw. Japanese woodworkers pull. Here's why both are right."
   - *Creators:* Samurai Carpenter
   - *Best for:* Technique comparison, cultural context, history-of-craft articles

9. **The Experimental Artist** — "What if?" Creative risk as instruction.
   - *Written voice:* "What happens if you laminate 47 layers of dyed plywood and then turn it on a lathe?"
   - *Creators:* Michael Alm, Laura Kampf
   - *Best for:* Advanced technique, design innovation, creative challenges

10. **The Principled Purist** — Constraints as philosophy. Less is more.
    - *Written voice:* "You don't need a table saw for this. You don't even need electricity."
    - *Creators:* Wood By Wright, James Wright
    - *Best for:* Hand tool guides, slow craft content, mindfulness-meets-making

## Recommended Blend for Bespoke Woodcraft Studio

Based on the studio's existing voice (warm, calm, clear — "like a craftsman walking you through how something works"):

**Primary:** The Patient Master (60%)
**Secondary:** The Honest Maker (25%)
**Accent:** The Philosophical Tradesman (15%)

This produces: **"A skilled friend who knows their stuff, shares freely, admits mistakes, and occasionally reminds you why this craft matters."**

## Voice Element Mapping

| Element | What it sounds like | Example |
|---------|-------------------|---------|
| **Authority** | Specific details, not vague claims | "Set your marking gauge to 3/16" — not "set it to about a quarter inch" |
| **Warmth** | Anticipates the reader's feelings | "This part looks intimidating. It's actually the easiest step." |
| **Clarity** | Short sentences, active voice | "Clamp the board. Mark the line. Cut to the waste side." |
| **Humor** | Self-deprecating, never at reader's expense | "Ask me how I know this. Actually, don't. My left thumbnail will never be the same." |
| **Philosophy** | Connects technique to meaning | "Dovetails aren't just strong. They're a conversation between two pieces of wood." |
