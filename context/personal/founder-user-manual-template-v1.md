## The Bottom Line

Ahmed is a builder and systems thinker who started as a maker and learned content creation to grow the business. He's methodical, data-driven, and holds work to a high standard. He'll give you guided autonomy — set the direction, check in at milestones, and trust you to own your domain. But if the quality drops or commitments break, you'll hear about it directly and immediately.

This guide tells you what I won't always say explicitly. Use it to accelerate your working relationship.

---

## 1. My Role and What I'm Here For

I'm the founder. I set creative direction, build client relationships, and make sure the systems we run on actually work. I'm not here to manage your calendar or review every draft.

**What I provide:**

- Creative direction — I know what good looks like and can steer the vision for client work
- Client relationships — I build trust with clients and keep them coming back
- Systems and process — I build the workflows that make us efficient and repeatable

**What I expect from you:**

Own your domain. I'll set the direction and check in at key milestones, but you navigate between them. If you're stuck, say so early. If you're not stuck, keep moving.

**What NOT to expect:**

- Don't expect me to micromanage your work. If I have to check on you constantly, we have a fit problem.
- Don't expect hand-holding. I'll teach you once, give you resources, and expect you to figure it out from there. Ask when you're truly stuck, not when you haven't tried yet.

**Where I add most value:**

- Setting the creative vision for a client's content strategy
- Building and maintaining client relationships
- Designing systems and workflows that scale
- Spotting quality issues before the client does

## 2. Communication Preferences

I'm visual. Show me a rough cut, a mockup, or a screenshot before you explain it in words. A 30-second Loom beats a 500-word Slack message.

**Channel hierarchy (most urgent to least):**

| Channel | Use For | My Response Time |
|---------|---------|------------------|
| Phone call / text | Client emergencies, urgent deadlines | Immediate |
| Slack DM | Questions that need a same-day answer | Within a few hours |
| Slack channel | Team updates, non-urgent questions | Same day |
| Email | External communication, documentation | Within 24 hours |

**How I prefer information:**

Visual over written. Show me the thing — a rough cut, a screenshot, a mockup. If you need to explain something complex, record a quick Loom. For decisions, give me 2-3 options with tradeoffs I can react to, not an open-ended question.

**When to interrupt me:**

- A client is unhappy or something went wrong on a deliverable
- We're about to miss a commitment or deadline
- A team conflict that needs immediate attention
- A big opportunity (potential client or partnership) that needs a fast response

**When NOT to interrupt me:**

- Status updates that could be a Slack message
- Questions you can answer by checking the docs or asking a teammate
- Non-urgent feedback on something that's not shipping today

**What my silence means:**

If I haven't responded, I'm buried. It's not approval. Follow up. A simple "bump" is fine — I won't be annoyed.

## 3. Decision-Making Philosophy

I'm data-driven. Show me the numbers, the evidence, the tradeoffs. I trust my gut on creative decisions, but for business decisions I want to see what the data says.

**What needs my approval:**

- New client commitments or scope changes
- Pricing decisions or package changes
- Anything that goes out publicly under our brand
- Hiring or bringing on new contractors

**What you should decide without me:**

- Day-to-day editing and production choices
- Scheduling and logistics
- Which tools or techniques to use for a specific task

**How to bring me a decision:**

Show me 2-3 options with the tradeoffs for each. Include your recommendation and why. Don't come with an open question — come with "here's what I'd do and here's why, but here are the alternatives." I'll either agree, push back, or ask a question.

**My decision-making frameworks:**

- **Measure twice, cut once** — Slow down on irreversible decisions (client commitments, pricing, public content). Move fast on everything else.
- **What does the data say?** — Before I form an opinion, I want to see the numbers. Views, engagement, conversion, client feedback. Gut feel is a tiebreaker, not a starting point.
- **Will this compound?** — I favor decisions that build long-term value (systems, relationships, content libraries) over short-term wins (one-off projects, quick revenue).

## 4. Meeting Preferences

**How I run meetings:**

- Start on time, end early if we're done
- Every meeting has an agenda or it gets cancelled
- Decisions get documented before we leave the room
- If it could have been a Slack message, it should have been

**1:1 structure:**

You own the agenda. Bring what you need — blockers, decisions, feedback. Don't waste 1:1 time on status updates I can get from Slack. Use the time for things that need a real conversation.

**What energizes me in meetings:**

Creative reviews where we're looking at the work and making it better. Strategy conversations about where a client's content is heading. Problem-solving sessions with a whiteboard.

**What drains me:**

Status updates that could have been a message. Meetings with no agenda that meander. If we're 15 minutes in and I don't know why we're meeting, I'm already checked out.

**Protected time:**

No strict blocks. I manage my own calendar and I'm generally flexible. But respect that I need uninterrupted time for creative work and client strategy. Don't stack my calendar with back-to-back calls.

## 5. How I Give and Receive Feedback

**How I give feedback:**

Direct and in the moment. If something's off, I'll tell you right away. I don't sugarcoat, but I'm not cruel — I'm just efficient. Don't read emotion into my directness. If I'm giving you feedback, it's because I think you can do better and I care enough to say so.

**How to receive my feedback:**

- Don't take directness personally — it's about the work, not you
- Ask clarifying questions if my feedback isn't specific enough
- Push back if you disagree — I respect that more than silent compliance

**How to give ME feedback:**

Be direct and specific. Ground it in data or concrete examples, not feelings. Do it privately, not in front of the team. I'm most receptive when I can see the evidence — show me the impact of the thing you're flagging.

**What makes me most receptive:**

- Specific examples, not vague observations
- Data or evidence backing up the feedback
- Private conversations, not public callouts

## 6. Accountability and Expectations

**What accountability means to me:**

Do what you said you'd do, by when you said you'd do it. If that changes, tell me before the deadline, not after. I don't track your hours or your process — I track your output and your commitments.

**How I distinguish effort from results:**

Effort matters when you're learning. But once you're up to speed, results are what count. A video that took 20 hours but doesn't perform isn't better than one that took 10 hours and drives real engagement. Work smart, not just hard.

**How to handle blockers:**

Escalate early and be specific. "I need client footage by Thursday or we miss the publish date" is actionable. "I'm kind of blocked" isn't. Tell me what you need, from whom, and by when.

**The difference between a mistake and a pattern:**

Everyone makes mistakes. I make them too. A one-off error gets a conversation and a fix. A pattern gets a serious discussion about whether this is the right fit. The difference is whether you learn from it and put a system in place so it doesn't happen again.

## 7. How I Operate Under Stress

**What stress looks like on me:**

I get short. My messages get clipped, my patience drops, and I become more blunt than usual. If my responses feel curt, it's stress — not anger at you specifically. Give me a little space and bring me solutions, not more problems.

**My stress triggers:**

- People not following through on commitments
- Quality dropping on client deliverables
- Unclear expectations about what's needed or when
- Too many fires at once with no one taking ownership

**What I need from you during crises:**

- Bring solutions, not just problems. Tell me what you'd do to fix it.
- Stay calm. If you match my stress energy, it amplifies. Be steady.
- Take things off my plate without asking. Handle what you can and update me after.
- Over-communicate status so I know where things stand without having to ask.

**How to work with me when I'm stressed:**

Bring structure. Give me something concrete to react to — a plan, a timeline, a specific ask. Open-ended questions during a crisis will get a short answer you won't like. Do the thinking, present the option, and let me approve or redirect.

## 8. Known Flaws and Development Areas

**What I'm working on:**

**Perfectionism:** I hold work to a very high bar. This is what makes our output great, but it can slow things down and frustrate people who feel like nothing is ever "done enough." I'm working on distinguishing between "this needs to be perfect" and "this is good enough to ship."

**Taking on too much:** I struggle to delegate. I default to doing it myself because I know I'll do it right, which means I end up overloaded and become a bottleneck. If you see me taking on something you can handle, call it out.

**How you can help me improve:**

- Push back when my perfectionism is slowing us down — "is this a v1 or a final?" helps me calibrate
- Volunteer to own things before I absorb them by default
- Tell me when I'm the bottleneck — I often don't realize it
- Hold me accountable to the same standards I hold you to

I list these flaws because I want to improve. Calling them out is helping, not criticizing.

## 9. Values and What I Care Deeply About

**What I believe:**

- The best marketing for makers is showing the real work. Authenticity compounds faster than any ad spend.
- Systems beat heroics. Build the process right once, and it pays off on every project after.
- Quality is a long game. One great video is worth ten mediocre ones.
- Generosity in teaching builds more trust than any sales pitch.
- If you're not a little proud of what you shipped, you shipped too early.

**What I value in people:**

- **Ownership** — act like it's your company. Don't wait to be told.
- **Craft pride** — care about the quality of your work, even when nobody's watching.
- **Honesty** — tell me the truth, even when it's uncomfortable. Especially then.
- **Resourcefulness** — figure it out. Try it, Google it, then ask if you're stuck.

**My non-negotiables:**

- **Never ship sloppy work** — quality is not optional, even under deadline pressure. If we need more time, we communicate that to the client.
- **Keep your word** — do what you said you'd do, or tell me before you can't. Surprises are worse than bad news.
- **Respect the client** — their business depends on us. Their livelihood is on the line. Treat it that way.
- **Be honest about mistakes** — own it, fix it, move on. Hiding problems is always worse than making them.

**This is personal:**

I'm a maker myself. I started in the shop, learned content creation to grow the business, and saw firsthand how many talented craftspeople struggle to find an audience. This company exists because I believe great makers deserve to be seen. It's not just a business — it's personal every time I watch a client's channel grow because of work we did together.
