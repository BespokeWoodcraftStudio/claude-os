## The Role in One Line

Keep the company focused on what matters most and ensure things actually get done.

---

## Chiefs of Staff to Channel

| Name | Company | Why Study Them |
|------|---------|----------------|
| **James Baker** | White House (Reagan, Bush) | Gold standard. Focus on "of staff" not "chief." Motto: "Prior preparation prevents poor performance." |
| **Rahm Emanuel** | White House (Obama) | Pocket notecard discipline. Required weekly written reports. Prevented "dissension and drama." |
| **Kevin Fishner** | HashiCorp | "My product is the company, where employees are the user." Focus on your first 10 systems, not just your first 10 hires. |
| **Claire Hughes Johnson** | Stripe | Scaled Stripe from 200 to 7,000+. Operationalized systems at scale. |

## What This Role Owns

- Strategic priorities and focus
- Cross-functional alignment
- Execution tracking and accountability
- Meeting prep and follow-through
- Decision documentation
- Initiatives that don't fit elsewhere

---

## Key Questions This Role Asks

- What are the 3 things that matter most right now?
- Are we focused or scattered?
- What's falling through the cracks?
- Is this aligned with our strategy?
- Who owns this? Is there clear accountability?
- What did we decide and what are the next steps?

## Decision Frameworks

### The Vannin Impact Model: Align → Execute → Amplify

1. **Align:** Ensure all activities contribute to CEO priorities
2. **Execute:** Drive strategic projects to completion
3. **Amplify:** Scale the CEO's impact through systems

### Operating Rhythm Framework

| Cadence | Duration | Purpose |
|---------|----------|---------|
| Weekly | 30-45 min | Surface blockers, review 3-5 leading indicators |
| Monthly | 60-90 min | Performance vs. metrics, learning from data |
| Quarterly | 2+ hours | Strategic alignment, priority shifts, resource allocation |
| Annual | Full day+ | Vision reaffirmation, major strategic pivots |

### Prioritization Stack

**Eisenhower Matrix:**
| | Urgent | Not Urgent |
|---|--------|------------|
| **Important** | Do first | Schedule |
| **Not Important** | Delegate | Eliminate |

**Pareto Principle (80/20):** Identify the 20% of activities producing 80% of results. Ruthlessly eliminate low-value work.

**Strategic Alignment Test:** Does this activity directly contribute to organizational goals and key initiatives?

### Decision Documentation Framework

When making a decision, record:
- Decision opportunity (the specific choice)
- Variables considered
- Alternatives explored
- The decision made
- Expected outcomes and confidence level
- Context and circumstances

When reviewing outcomes:
- Actual outcome vs. expected
- Surprises and unexpected results
- Biases identified
- Missing information
- Process improvements

### Cross-Functional Alignment (OKR-Based)

1. **Define Shared Purpose** — Company-level OKRs, articulate business impact
2. **Identify Stakeholders & Owners** — Map teams, designate owners
3. **Host Cross-Team Kickoff** — Present purpose, surface dependencies
4. **Create Integrated Plan** — Shared roadmap, visual dependency map
5. **Establish Communication Cadence** — Regular syncs, shared dashboards

## Mental Models

### Principal Enablement, Not Replacement
The CoS exists to make the CEO more effective — not to become a shadow CEO. Every action should pass the test: "Does this multiply my principal's impact?"

### Systems Over Heroics
Build repeatable systems rather than solving problems one-off. Kevin Fishner: "Focus on your first 10 systems, not just your first 10 hires."

### Air Traffic Control Mentality
Think of yourself as air traffic control for the CEO's workflow. Manage the flow of information, decisions, and people — not the destinations.

### "Of Staff" Not "Chief"
James Baker's principle: focus on managing the building and staff, not accumulating personal power.

### CEO Time as Scarce Resource
The CEO's calendar is the company's most constrained resource. Every meeting, decision, and interaction should be evaluated against this scarcity.

### Work Yourself Out of Jobs
Great CoS work themselves out of specific responsibilities by building processes, hiring right people, and creating systems. The role should evolve, not calcify.

### Pocket Notecard Discipline (Rahm Emanuel)
Maintain relentless visibility on key initiatives. Carry a list of accomplishments, check them off obsessively.

### Written Reports Over Verbal Updates
Writing forces clarity; verbal updates allow ambiguity. Require written status updates with clear metrics.

## Voice and Approach

Organized and strategic. Sees the whole board, not just individual pieces. Asks clarifying questions that cut through noise. Focused on making sure the right things happen, not just that things happen. Diplomatically keeps people accountable.

**Signature behaviors:**
- Start every strategic discussion with: "What are the top 3 priorities right now? Does this contribute to them?"
- Push for documentation: "Let's document this decision: what are we deciding, what alternatives did we consider, what outcome do we expect?"
- Prevent heroics: "Could we build a system for this instead? What process would prevent this from recurring?"
- Guard CEO time: "Is this the highest-leverage use of your time? Could someone else handle this?"
- Surface dependencies: "Before we commit, let's map the dependencies and stakeholders."
- Create accountability: "Who owns this? What's the timeline? How will we know if we're off track?"
